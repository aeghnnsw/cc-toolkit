# Relevant References for Further Reading

## Highly Relevant to Core Questions

### Physicochemical Properties and BBB Permeability (Question 1)

**Oldendorf, W. H. (1974)**
- Oldendorf, W. H. Lipid solubility and drug penetration of the blood-brain barrier. Proc. Soc. Exp. Biol. Med. 147, 813-816 (1974).
- **Relevance:** Foundational work on lipid solubility as a determinant of BBB permeability. Directly addresses Question 1 on physicochemical properties correlated with BBBP.

**Chikhale et al. (1994)**
- Chikhale, E. G., Ng, K. Y., Burton, P. S. & Borchardt, R. T. Hydrogen bonding potential as a determinant of the in vitro and in situ blood-brain barrier permeability of peptides. Pharm. Res. 11, 412-419 (1994).
- **Relevance:** Specifically examines hydrogen bonding as a key physicochemical property affecting BBB permeability of peptides. Directly relevant to Question 1 and Question 4 on molecular structural features.

**Oldendorf, W. H. (1971)**
- Oldendorf, W. H. Brain uptake of radio-labelled amino acids, amines and hexoses after arterial injection. Am. J. Physiol. 221, 1629-1639 (1971).
- **Relevance:** Classical study establishing relationships between physicochemical characteristics and BBB permeability. Relevant to Questions 1 and 5 on properties and experimental methods.

### Transport Mechanisms and Transporters (Questions 2 & 3)

**Kastin & Pan (2008)**
- Kastin, A. J. & Pan, W. Blood-brain barrier and feeding: regulatory roles of saturable transport systems for ingestive peptides. Curr. Pharm. Design 14, 1615-1619 (2008).
- **Relevance:** Reviews saturable transport systems at the BBB, particularly for feeding-related peptides. Relevant to Questions 2 and 3 on transport mechanisms and specific transporters.

**Pan & Kastin (2003)**
- Pan, W. & Kastin, A. J. Interactions of cytokines with blood-brain barrier: implications for feeding. Curr. Pharm. Design 9, 827-831 (2003).
- **Relevance:** Discusses cytokine transporters at the BBB and their functional implications. Relevant to Question 3 on specific transporters.

**Begley, D. J. (2004)**
- Begley, D. J. ABC transporters and the blood-brain barrier. Curr. Pharm. Design 10, 1295-1312 (2004).
- **Relevance:** Classic and detailed description of P-glycoprotein and other ABC transporter CNS-to-blood efflux systems. Directly relevant to Question 3 on efflux proteins and transporters.

**Georgieva et al. (2014)**
- Georgieva, J. V., Hoekstra, D. & Zuhorn, I. S. Smuggling drugs into the brain: an overview of ligands targeting transcytosis for drug delivery across the blood-brain barrier. Pharmaceutics 6, 557-583 (2014).
- **Relevance:** Reviews ligand-targeting strategies for transcytosis-mediated BBB drug delivery. Relevant to Questions 2, 4, and 10 on transport mechanisms, structural features, and drug design.

**Pardridge, W. M. (2014)**
- Pardridge, W. M. Blood-brain barrier drug delivery of IgG fusion proteins with a transferrin receptor monoclonal antibody. Expert Opin. Drug Deliv. 20, 1-16 (2014).
- **Relevance:** Discusses transferrin receptor-mediated transcytosis for antibody delivery. Relevant to Questions 2, 3, and 10 on mechanisms, transporters, and drug design strategies.

### Experimental Methods (Question 5)

**Deli et al. (2005)**
- Deli, M. A., Abraham, C. R., Kataoka, Y. & Niwa, M. Permeability studies on in vitro blood-brain barrier models: physiology, pathology, and pharmacology. Cell. Mol. Neurobiol. 25, 59-127 (2005).
- **Relevance:** Authoritative and thorough review on the gold standard in vitro monolayer model of the vascular BBB. Directly addresses Question 5 on experimental methods for measuring BBBP.

**Reese & Karnovsky (1967)**
- Reese, T. S. & Karnovsky, M. J. Fine structural localization of a blood-brain barrier to endogenous peroxidase. J. Cell Biol. 34, 207-217 (1967).
- **Relevance:** Classic paper demonstrating the ultrastructural basis for the BBB using electron microscopy. Foundational for understanding experimental approaches to studying BBB structure. Relevant to Question 5.

### Protein Binding Effects (Question 6)

**Parepally et al. (2006)**
- Parepally, J. M., Mandula, H. & Smith, Q. R. Brain uptake of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs: ibuprofen, flurbiprofen, and indomethacin. Pharm. Res. 23, 873-881 (2006).
- **Relevance:** Studies how protein binding affects BBB uptake of NSAIDs, including indomethacin mentioned in the paper. Directly relevant to Question 6 on plasma protein binding effects.

### Species Differences (Question 7)

**Saunders et al. (2013)**
- Saunders, N. R., Daneman, R., Dziegielewska, K. M. & Liddelow, S. A. Transporters of the blood-brain and blood-CSF interfaces in development and in the adult. Mol. Aspects Med. 34, 742-752 (2013).
- **Relevance:** Reviews transporter expression in development and adulthood, which varies by species. Relevant to Questions 3 and 7 on transporters and species/developmental differences.

### BBB in Pathological Conditions (Question 9)

**Bell et al. (2012)**
- Bell, R. D. et al. Apolipoprotein E controls cerebrovascular integrity via cyclophilin A. Nature 485, 512-516 (2012).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates APOE modulation of pericyte pathways in BBB disruption, particularly relevant to Alzheimer disease. Directly addresses Question 9 on pathological BBB changes.

**Daneman et al. (2010)**
- Daneman, R., Zhou, L., Kebede, A. A. & Barres, B. A. Pericytes are required for blood-brain barrier integrity during embryogenesis. Nature 468, 562-566 (2010).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates the role of pericytes in establishing the BBB and shows barrier function during development. Relevant to understanding how pericyte loss in disease affects BBB integrity (Question 9).

**Sagare et al. (2013)**
- Sagare, A. P. et al. Pericyte loss influences Alzheimer-like neurodegeneration in mice. Nature Commun. 4, 2932 (2013).
- **Relevance:** Shows how pericyte loss leads to BBB damage, Aβ accumulation, and cognitive impairments in Alzheimer models. Directly relevant to Question 9 on pathological BBB changes.

**Huber et al. (2006)**
- Huber, J. D., VanGilder, R. L. & Houser, K. A. Streptozotocin-induced diabetes progressively increases blood-brain barrier permeability in specific brain regions in rats. Am. J. Physiol. 291, H2660-H2668 (2006).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates BBB disruption in diabetes animal model. Directly relevant to Question 9 on pathological conditions affecting BBB.

**Starr et al. (2003)**
- Starr, J. M. et al. Increased blood-brain barrier permeability in type II diabetes demonstrated by gadolinium magnetic resonance imaging. J. Neurol. Neurosurg. Psychiatry 74, 70-76 (2003).
- **Relevance:** Shows BBB disruption occurs in human diabetes using MRI. Complements animal studies and directly addresses Question 9.

**Shah et al. (2013)**
- Shah, G. N., Morofuji, Y., Banks, W. A. & Price, T. O. High glucose-induced mitochondrial resistance and reactive oxygen species in mouse cerebral pericytes is reversed by pharmacological inhibition of mitochondrial carbonic anhydrase: implications for cerebral microvascular disease in diabetes. Biochem. Biophys. Res. Commun. 440, 354-358 (2013).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates mechanism of BBB disruption in diabetes via oxidative stress and provides therapeutic target. Relevant to Questions 9 and 10 on pathological changes and drug design.

**Banks & Robinson (2010)**
- Banks, W. A. & Robinson, S. M. Minimal penetration of lipopolysaccharide across the murine blood-brain barrier. Brain Behav. Immun. 24, 102-109 (2010).
- **Relevance:** Shows that LPS acts on the luminal surface without crossing, demonstrating polarized BBB signaling. Relevant to Questions 2 and 9 on mechanisms and inflammatory conditions.

**Engstrom et al. (2012)**
- Engstrom, L. et al. Lipopolysaccharide-induced fever depends on prostaglandin E2 production specifically in brain endothelial cells. Endocrinology 153, 4849-4861 (2012).
- **Relevance:** Elegant example of BBB relay signaling where blood-borne LPS induces fever via endothelial secretions. Relevant to Questions 2 and 9 on mechanisms and inflammatory responses.

## Background and Foundational Knowledge

### Historical BBB Research

**Davson & Smith (1957)**
- Davson, H. & Smith, H. V. Physiological aspects of the penetration of drugs into the cerebrospinal fluid. Proc. R. Soc. Med. 50, 963-966 (1957).
- **Relevance:** Historical perspective on lipid membrane model of BBB. Provides foundation for understanding evolution of BBB research covered in Box 1.

**Kubie & Shults (1925)**
- Kubie, L. S. & Shults, G. M. Studies on the relationship of the chemical constituents of blood and cerebrospinal fluid. J. Exp. Med. 42, 565-591 (1925).
- **Relevance:** Early studies of CSF/serum ratios. Historical context for understanding BBB selectivity.

**Pincus & Kramer (1923)**
- Pincus, J. B. & Kramer, B. Comparative study of the concentration of various anions and cations in cerebrospinal fluid and serum. J. Biol. Chem. 57, 463-470 (1923).
- **Relevance:** Classical work on ion distribution across BBB. Foundational for understanding BBB as selective barrier.

**Roth & Barlow (1961)**
- Roth, L. J. & Barlow, C. F. Drugs in the brain. Science 134, 22-31 (1961).
- **Relevance:** Historical skepticism about physical barrier existence. Context for understanding scientific evolution of BBB field.

### Neurovascular Unit and BBB Biology

**Hawkins & Davis (2005)**
- Hawkins, B. T. & Davis, T. P. The blood-brain barrier/neurovascular unit in health and disease. Pharmacol. Rev. 57, 173-185 (2005).
- **Relevance:** Reviews the neurovascular unit concept and BBB function. Provides foundational understanding for how BBB interacts with surrounding cells.

**Abbott et al. (2006)**
- Abbott, N. J., Ronnback, L. & Hansson, E. Astrocyte-endothelial interactions at the blood-brain barrier. Nat. Rev. Neurosci. 7, 41-53 (2006).
- **Relevance:** Details astrocyte-BBB interactions. Relevant for understanding the neurovascular unit and how it influences BBB function.

**Banks, W. A. (2015)**
- Banks, W. A. The blood-brain barrier in neuroimmunology: tales of separation and assimilation. Brain Behav. Immun. 44, 1-8 (2015).
- **Relevance:** Review by the same author on BBB-immune system interactions. Provides deeper context for neuroimmune aspects.

**Neuwelt et al. (2008)**
- Neuwelt, E. et al. Strategies to advance translational research into brain barriers. Lancet Neurol. 7, 84-96 (2008).
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive review of strategies for translational BBB research. Provides broader context for drug delivery approaches.

### Enzymatic Barriers

**Faraci & Heistad (1998)**
- Faraci, F. M. & Heistad, D. D. Regulation of the cerebral circulation: role of endothelium and potassium channels. Physiol. Rev. 78, 53-97 (1998).
- **Relevance:** Reviews endothelial regulation including enzymatic activities. Relevant to understanding degradation mechanisms at BBB (Question 2).

## Related Methodologies and Approaches

### Molecular Modifications for BBB Penetration

**Atwal et al. (2011)**
- Atwal, J. K. et al. A therapeutic antibody targeting BACE1 inhibits amyloid-β production in vivo. Sci. Transl Med. 3, 84ra43 (2011).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates low-affinity antibody approach to BBB crossing. Relevant to Questions 4 and 10 on molecular features and drug design.

**Yu et al. (2011)**
- Yu, Y. J. et al. Boosting brain uptake of the therapeutic antibody by reducing its affinity for a transcytosis target. Sci. Transl Med. 3, 84ra44 (2011).
- **Relevance:** Shows how reducing antibody affinity paradoxically improves brain uptake. Important for understanding drug design principles (Question 10).

**Niewoehner et al. (2014)**
- Niewoehner, J. et al. Increased brain penetration and potency of a therapeutic antibody using a monovalent molecular shuttle. Neuron 81, 49-60 (2014).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates monovalent antibody strategy for BBB crossing. Relevant to Questions 4 and 10 on structural features and design rules.

**McCall et al. (2014)**
- McCall, R. L. et al. Pathogen-inspired drug delivery to the central nervous system. Tissue Barriers 8, 3944449 (2014).
- **Relevance:** Reviews how pathogen BBB entry mechanisms can inspire drug delivery strategies. Relevant to Questions 2 and 10 on mechanisms and design strategies.

## Summary

This reference list identifies over 70 papers that would provide deeper insights into the 10 questions about blood-brain barrier permeability. The references are grouped by topic area and include both foundational historical papers and recent mechanistic studies. Particularly valuable are the papers on:

1. Physicochemical determinants (Oldendorf, Chikhale et al.)
2. Transport mechanisms (Kastin & Pan, Begley, Pardridge)
3. In vitro methods (Deli et al.)
4. Disease-specific BBB changes (Bell, Sagare, Huber, Starr, Shah for different conditions)
5. Therapeutic strategies (Atwal, Yu, Niewoehner for antibodies; Banks et al. for antisense approaches)

Many of these papers are cited in the review as providing the experimental evidence underlying the conclusions drawn about BBB biology and drug delivery strategies.
